01 — Research Framing

Imagination-Based Approaches for
Planning and Governing the African City

01.01

The Central Argument

This presentation draws on an ongoing PhD research project examining how imagination-based approaches can reframe the planning and governance of African cities.

The core provocation is deceptively simple: whose imagination is being used when we design, govern, and plan the city?

PhD Research

01.02

Why Creative Economies?

Creative economies are driven by culture, identity, innovation, storytelling, design, music, art, fashion, digital media, and everyday creativity.

In African cities, they often emerge within informal, fluid, and adaptive environments that traditional planning systems overlook.

Creative Economies

01.03

What Futures Literacy Contributes

Futures Literacy is the ability to understand and use the future to make better decisions in the present. It helps people recognise how assumptions, fears, hopes, and dominant narratives shape the way we imagine tomorrow.

Imagination becomes a tool for exploring alternative possibilities beyond what currently exists.

Futures Literacy

02 — The Persistent Paradox

African Cities and Urban Planning:
Whose Imagination Matters?

02.01

The Persistent Paradox

African cities are complex, fluid, and constantly evolving (Simone, 2004). Yet the planning frameworks applied to them are frequently imported, static, and ill-fitted to the realities they claim to govern.

Urban Theory

02.02

Gray Spaces

Gray spaces are informal, temporary, or semi-legal developments within contemporary city regions. They exist in a legal twilight zone: neither fully integrated into official city master plans ("white spaces") nor entirely condemned or cleared ("black spaces") — Yiftachel (2009).

They are, in other words, the spaces where most urban life actually happens.

Spatial Theory

02.03

The Tension

We speak of "context-sensitive planning" but in reality:

  • Practice often does not reflect lived urban realities
  • Planning systems remain institutionally and conceptually misaligned
  • Education continues to reproduce Eurocentric paradigms
Critical Planning

03 — Core Provocation

Contention of Space

Whose visions shape the city, and whose ways of living are treated as disorder? This space reveals the tension between formal planning systems and the everyday practices through which people create livelihoods, belonging, mobility, and survival in the African city.

03.01

The Planned vs the Lived

The African city is not only planned through policy and infrastructure, but through everyday acts of survival, creativity, movement, and negotiation.

Urban Life

03.02

Multiple Coexisting Realities

Urban space in African cities is contested — multiple realities, economies, and imaginations coexist. This challenges dominant assumptions of order, legitimacy, and planning, inviting reflection on whose futures are centred, and whose are marginalised in urban governance.

04 — Creative Economies

Creating Value from the Margins

Beyond informality, this space represents an active creative and economic ecosystem shaped by mobility, exchange, improvisation, and lived experience — forms of urban intelligence often overlooked in conventional planning frameworks.

04.01

What Creative Economies Generate

Creative economies create new forms of value, livelihoods, entrepreneurship, and social connection within urban spaces.

They reveal how communities continuously imagine, negotiate, and reshape urban life from the ground up.

By 2046

Creative economies have become global systems of imagination, where culture, storytelling, digital creativity, and identity shape economic and political influence as much as traditional industries.

While creativity connects the world, tensions emerge around cultural ownership, authenticity, and whose imagination dominates global platforms.

05 — Methodological Framework

Futures Literacy Labs

05.00

What Are Futures Literacy Labs?

Futures Literacy Labs (FLLs) are participatory foresight processes developed under UNESCO's Futures Literacy initiative. Introduced by Riel Miller, Futures Literacy is understood as a core capability that enables individuals and communities to recognise how the future shapes present perceptions and actions (Miller, 2015; 2018).

FLLs are designed as interactive, collective learning spaces that challenge taken-for-granted assumptions about the future. Through structured exploration, participants engage with multiple alternative futures, surfacing hidden biases and expanding how social, economic, and cultural dynamics are understood. In doing so, FLLs open up new possibilities for thinking, planning, and decision-making in complex and uncertain contexts.

05.01

Lab Phase 1: Tacit to Explicit

Participants uncover multiple embedded assumptions about their futures and hopefully realise that they can challenge or let go of original assumptions. The process harnesses collective intelligence, inspiring each other to make implicit premises explicit.

Reveal

05.02

Lab Phase 2: Reframing

The objective is to imagine and describe a future from an unfamiliar point of departure: a scenario that is neither probable nor desirable, which provokes and plays with a new set of anticipatory assumptions.

Imagine & Invent

05.03

Lab Phase 3: New Questions

Participants return to the present, reassessing their current perceptions and raising new questions that can influence future action.

Ask

05.04

Lab Phase 4: Next Steps

The objective is to consolidate group questions, insights, and lessons, identifying possible action for future change.

Acquire

06 — Mini FLL Activity

The Future of Creative Economies

Phase 1a — Probable Futures (2046)

What do you think is the probable future for creative economies? What do you rationally expect will happen, based on data, trends, and realistic assessment (not wishes or fears)?

Each person writes 3 sticky notes → round robin sharing → scenario clustering → pick one that is most mutually interesting to represent the group.

Phase 1b — Preferable Futures (2046)

What would your dream wish or hope for? What is your vision of a good future for creative economies? This is the future we want to happen, based on values, aspirations, and desires.

Each person writes 3 sticky notes → round robin sharing → scenario clustering → pick one that is most mutually interesting to represent the group.

Phase 2 — Causal Layered Analysis

Using the selected probable future as a headline, map out the iceberg at each layer: 1. Selected Headline, 2. Systems (PESTLE), 3. Worldviews, 4. Myths and Metaphors.

Based on Inayatullah's Causal Layered Analysis framework.

06.01

Why This Matters for African Urban Planning

  • African cities are dynamic, adaptive, and constantly reshaped through culture and everyday creativity.
  • Futures Literacy Labs help communities and practitioners rethink how creativity, culture, and imagination contribute to urban transformation.
  • They support more inclusive, context-sensitive, and future-conscious approaches to planning and governance.
  • They position creative economies not as secondary industries, but as central drivers of social, spatial, and economic futures.
